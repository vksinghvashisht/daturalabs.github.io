Why I Fired My
Private Wealth Manager

Twelve years of capital formation. One conversation that ended it. What private banking actually sells, what it actually costs, and what I built when I decided I didn't need it anymore.

By VK Singh Vashisht
March 2026
14 min read

There is a chapter in the Gospel of Matthew — chapter 21, verse 13 — where Jesus walks into the temple in Jerusalem, finds it full of moneychangers and merchants, overturns their tables, and says: "My house shall be called a house of prayer, but you have made it a den of thieves."

I think about that scene every time someone asks me why I left my private wealth manager.

Private banking is supposed to be about stewardship. About counsel. About sitting across from someone who understands your capital, your family, and your long-term interests, and helping you protect and grow what took a lifetime to build. That is what they sell. That is the story in every piece of marketing they put in front of you, in every meeting at their offices, in every leather-bound presentation deck.

What they actually deliver is access to themselves. The brand. The Rolodex. The sense that you are inside something exclusive and important. And in exchange for that feeling — for that particular form of institutional prestige — you pay a fee that compounds quietly against your net worth for years, sometimes for decades, and is almost never interrogated.

I interrogated it. That is what ended the relationship.

The Math Nobody Wants to Do

The first thing you notice, if you are paying attention, is that there is no single fee. There is an advisory fee, a management fee on each product, embedded costs inside the structured products they recommend, transaction fees when they move you in and out of positions, and then the tax drag from the way those positions are structured.

Add them up. Do the actual math. On a portfolio of reasonable size — say $15 to $25 million — the all-in cost, when you count every layer, is often between one and two percent of assets per year. Sometimes more.

That sounds like a small number. One percent. But one percent of $20 million is $200,000 per year. Compounded over ten years, at even a modest return, the fee drag is not a rounding error. It is a second home. It is a business. It is a material part of your family's financial future, quietly extracted every year for a service that, at its core, involves putting your money into products that the institution's product team has already approved for distribution.

The fee conversation is the conversation they are very good at not having.

They are expert at shifting the discussion to performance, to access, to the complexity of what they manage on your behalf — all of which serves to make the fee feel not just justified but small. That is not an accident. That is design.

What the Relationship Actually Is

I ran my family's natural gas operations for twelve years, from 2010 through 2022. That was not an easy time to be in natural gas. Gas prices were historically bad for most of that stretch. We survived because we understood our business — the actual economics, the actual risk, the actual levers — at a level that no outside advisor ever could.

When I moved into investment management after exiting the gas business, I brought the same instinct: understand the machine. Don't outsource the understanding. The outsourcing is where the margin goes — yours.

What I found, when I looked closely at the relationship, was that the machine was not designed for my interests. It was designed for theirs. The products they recommended most consistently were their own. The deal flow they showed me first was deal flow where they had a fee. The advice they gave me on timing — when to sell, when to hold, when to move capital — served their book as much as mine.

None of this is unique to any single institution. Every large wealth management firm operates this way because that is the business model. The model is asset retention and fee generation, dressed up as counsel and stewardship. Understanding this is not cynicism. It is just reading the incentive structure.

The Conversation That Ended It

There was a specific meeting. I will not tell you exactly when it was or who was in the room, because that is not the point. The point is what happened in the meeting.

I had been building — quietly, on my own — the capability to evaluate deals and manage capital directly. I had the legal structure in place: a single-client family office operating through a trust structure, which by statute does not require SEC registration as a registered investment adviser. I had the analytical tools. I had the frameworks. I had done the homework.

In this meeting, I asked a direct question about a specific allocation decision — why this product, why now, why this structure. The answer I got was fluent and professional and completely empty of actual reasoning. It was the answer of someone whose job is to retain assets, not to generate them.

I realized, sitting in that meeting, that I was not talking to a counselor. I was talking to a salesperson. A sophisticated one. A well-dressed one operating in a very expensive building. But a salesperson.

I walked out and started the transition.

What I Built Instead

The single-client family office structure is not complicated, but most people don't know it exists or don't understand that it is accessible to them. If you are managing capital for a single family — your family — you do not need a registered investment adviser. You operate through a trust or an LLC, you maintain direct custody of assets, and you make your own decisions.

The capability that private banking charges for is, in large part, now available through AI. The analytical layer — the deal screening, the market research, the due diligence structuring, the comparable analysis — can be built in-house, on frontier models, for a fraction of what institutions charge to provide it. This is not a future state. It is the current state. I built it. It works.

The things that large wealth management firms actually do well — and I will be fair: there are some — are network access, certain proprietary deal flow, and specific structured products that are genuinely difficult to access independently. If those things are valuable to you, the relationship may still make sense. But you should be paying for those specific things, explicitly, with full awareness of the cost. Not a bundled fee that obscures what you are actually receiving.

My framework for evaluating everything I look at now is what I call the Maya Framework — named for the Sanskrit concept of maya, meaning illusion. Most of what institutional wealth management sells you is maya: it looks like value, it has the aesthetics of expertise, but it does not compound in your favor the way the fees compound against you.

The Part Nobody Talks About

The hardest part of leaving — the part nobody talks about — is not the financial transition. It is the identity transition.

A major private banking relationship is a status signal. Being a client means something in the rooms where that kind of thing is tracked. Walking away from it means walking away from that signal. And for a lot of people — especially people who have worked very hard to accumulate the capital that gets them in the door in the first place — that signal feels important. Feels earned. Feels like the point.

It is maya.

The point is not being someone's client. The point is having capital that works for your family, on your terms, compounding in your direction. The brand is not the return. The brand is the fee.

I do not miss the meetings. I do not miss the presentations. I do not miss the fee drag. I miss nothing about the arrangement.

If you are thinking about making a similar move: do the math first. All of it. Then make the decision with your eyes open.

That is all I did.
